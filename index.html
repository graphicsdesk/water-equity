Community-focused projects push to make data more accessible to West Harlem residents for emergency planning

By Sophie Dau. Graphics by Adina Cazacu-De Luca

March 22, 2023

Shannon Binns / Staff Photographer

On 125th Street and Morningside Avenue, a camera looks down on the road, tracking flooding data. Although it has not yet recorded its first flood event, the sensor is one of many installed throughout New York City for a project that aims to use data and community evidence in studying flood impacts in the city.

This sensor is part of a pilot project of FloodNet, a research collaborative between the New York City government, New York University, and the City University of New York. The Mayor’s Office of Climate and Environmental Justice chose to install the sensor in West Harlem due to its position as an at-risk neighborhood both based on social vulnerability and analysis of Stormwater Flood Maps.

With increased flooding in West Harlem and throughout the city, both due to natural weather cycles and climate change, emergency preparedness has become a key concern. Communities have come together to provide local resources when city assistance becomes hard to receive. Although city agencies are committing to more community empowerment and support, the tasks of disseminating information and making data more accessible often fall on community organizations.

The FloodNet project places sensors which track flood data around the city and integrate resident reports of flooding from FloodWatch. Together, the data and reports will help create a picture of day-to-day city experiences with flooding, both normal and abnormal. According to Sara Eichner, a cartographer and designer who works with geographic information systems, this data set is particularly valuable because it forefronts on-the-ground experiences by allowing the resident reports to validate what the numbers show.

Eichner has been working on creating visualization tools for the FloodNet project. She aims to use her design work to meaningfully connect to social and environmental issues, and she identified FloodNet as a unique project that prioritizes community voices.

“This project is meant to kind of examine [emergency management alerts] and research around what people need to know about flooding and flood risk, what they want to know, and what’s useful, and how this project or the city might be able to provide that information in a clear and easy to understand way,” Eichner said.

FloodNet aims to install 500 sensors across the city by summer 2027. They determine locations for the sensors according to community requests as well as recommendations from the city while prioritizing equity for at-risk neighborhoods.

Hannah Burnett, a postdoctoral fellow with FloodNet who looks at different strategies for public engagement, has been working on community focus groups to refine the process of using flood data to help residents prepare for disasters. She said the original impetus for FloodNet came when the researchers found that communities often had to rely on individual witnesses and manually collected flood reports, and that they wanted to automate that process.

“What I’ve observed is a really true interest in the expertise of each other, which is just really amazing to see,” Burnett said regarding the collaboration between FloodNet, city agencies, and community organization. “I think there are of course differences, but it seems like the ultimate aims are in line with each other.”

Data is a key part of long-term emergency management. Ryan Hagen, a sociology professor at Columbia who studies institutional disaster risk management in New York City, explained that one of the major responsibilities of city planners is to preemptively map this data so that both responders and residents can know in advance the risks each neighborhood faces in the wake of a disaster.

“People aren’t left scrambling to figure out if there’s flooding because of a hurricane, where might there be toxic chemicals leaking out of a building, or where might there be radiological materials from a hospital or a research institution or whatever that might be involved in a fire, or any of these things,” Hagen said. “GIS data is incredibly important to know where things are and how they interconnect so that you can unravel, or you can kind of untangle, that mess when something goes wrong.”

Depending on the data used, the risk flooding poses West Harlem can look very different. The floodplain data, which the city has used for climate preparedness, puts West Harlem at a low risk. Yet, 311 maps show residents well outside the zone of flood risk experiencing water-related emergencies.

Additionally, the floodplain risk does not account for the built environment, which is what Spatial Equity NYC aims to address. By calculating the permeable surface area, which West Harlem ranks 51st out of the city's 59 districts, this report emphasizes the heightened risk of stormwater flooding due to a lack of infrastructure to properly drain water.

According to Annie Carforo, the Climate Justice Campaigns Coordinator for the environmental advocacy organization WE ACT for Environmental Justice, the data helps residents gain an understanding of their neighborhood in climate-related emergencies.

“It gives people the opportunity to really hone in on, where do I live? Where do I frequent? Where do I visit? Where do I need to go for work? What subway do I use, and am I going to be impacted by stormwater flooding?” she said.

Often, community organizations have had to take the initiative on making data accessible to residents. New York City has taken steps to address what some have called a history of leaving the community out of emergency management conversations. For example, the Office of Climate and Environmental Justice has worked with the Resilient Coastal Communities program at the Columbia Climate School to center community engagement in planning. Paul Gallay, the director of the Resilient Coast Communities program, feels that there i’s a shift toward more inclusive policies.

“I think that the agencies are working to do better, and they’re also working to create new systems,” Gallay said. “The city has a new initiative under the rubric of their climate knowledge exchange, where they’re working to create a Climate Strong Communities initiative. … It is the sort of project that could conceivably lead to empowerment and uplifting of communities in a way that hasn’t happened before.”

These efforts attempt to address the disparities in West Harlem emergency preparedness caused by differing environmental regulations. Recent storms in the past few years such as Hurricane Ida have revealed these historic health and environmental disparities. Hagen highlighted how tracking data is an important element of emergency management to identify the big picture for disasters that reveal a breakdown of infrastructure.

“You also see, inevitably, with all government programs and all administration, what you see is that infrastructure tends to get left behind and neglected in underserved communities that are underserved in other ways, and that’s just not unique to or characteristic of emergency management, but of modern urban administration,” he said. “That’s one of the realities that we face.”

New York City Emergency Management also created a database, the New York City Hazard Mitigation, which allows city residents to access maps of city-collected data about floodplains, extreme heat, stormwater resiliency, and more by community district. The agency has also produced a condensed version for each neighborhood. Melissa Umberger, director of recovery and risk under NYCEM, explained that the project’s goal was to allow all community members to understand what risks their neighborhood faces.

However, community organizations and equity-centered companies like WE ACT, FloodNet, and Transportation Alternatives have found that non-tech-savvy residents have had trouble accessing and understanding the maps and data.

To address these challenges, WE ACT committed to providing more accessible resources on emergency management. For example, WE ACT took stormwater flooding data from the city to create maps of Upper Manhattan and spread it among residents to help them answer these questions. Residents have found this detailed map to confirm their lived experiences—they know from years of experience “what street corners flood” and “which subways to avoid,” according to Carforo.

“What we were seeing where NYCEM’s effectiveness wears off is actually getting this information out into the community,” she said. “The other reality is that NYCEM has so much information and it can feel very overwhelming. They offer opportunities where they can give different workshops and seminars, but I think that a lot of people don’t want to talk or think about emergency planning because it scares them.”

Similarly, Jacob deCastro, a spokesperson for Transportation Alternatives—which recently collaborated on the Spatial Equity NYC—believes this report is a useful tool for the community.

“This tool is to empower people to advocate for spatial equity, and one of the best ways to tell stories through data,” he said. “We built this tool to give people access to data because the city puts out, you know, a lot of open data, but it’s not always in the most usable, the most readable, easily understandable formats. We really want this tool to do that, to provide data in a way that people can understand. You don’t need to be … the data analyst to understand.”

The Spatial Equity Report has been well-received so far, used by elected officials, policymakers, media outlets, and residents. Gallay, too, has emphasized the importance of community advocacy.

“Every community along New York City’s 520 miles of coastline is going to present a different set of challenges,” Gallay said. “The goal is to make sure those challenges are well understood by the agencies and completely factored into the design of whatever solutions and interventions those agencies tend to fund and build.”
